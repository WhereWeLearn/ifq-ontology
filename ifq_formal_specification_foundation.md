## Foundational Specification Draft

Version: Draft 0.1
Status: Conceptual Foundation
Purpose: Prevent conceptual drift during framework expansion

# 1. Introduction

The International Framework of Qualifications (IFQ) is an approximate international educational progression scale intended to compare mainstream educational stages across different national systems.

The framework is designed to support:

- international educational comparison,
- cross-system learner progression mapping,
- lesson recommendation,
- educational content classification,
- and globally portable instructional targeting.

The IFQ is being developed to support the mission of entity["organization","WhereWeLearn","education charity"] by enabling educational content to become discoverable and recommendable regardless of:

- nationality,
- educational background,
- learner age,
- institutional pathway,
- or educational system.

---

# 2. Core Philosophy

## 2.1 Comparative, Not Authoritative

The IFQ is a comparative framework.

It is NOT:

- a legal accreditation framework,
- a qualification authority,
- a curriculum replacement,
- or a definitive competency certification system.

The IFQ provides:

> approximate equivalence between educational progression stages across different educational systems.

Educational systems differ significantly in:

- curriculum structure,
- compulsory education age,
- educational philosophy,
- academic pacing,
- qualification structure,
- and instructional expectations.

Perfect equivalence is neither possible nor intended.

---

## 2.2 Progression-Oriented Rather Than Competency-Oriented

The IFQ primarily models:

- educational progression stage.

It does NOT attempt to universally determine:

- learner intelligence,
- academic mastery,
- or capability.

A learner may:

- study above their IFQ stage,
- study below their IFQ stage,
- or re-enter education later in life.

Example:

- an adult learner studying introductory algebra,
- and a younger learner studying the same material,

may both legitimately engage with the same IFQ-classified lesson.

The IFQ classifies:

- educational positioning,
- not learner worth or capability.

---

## 2.3 Approximate Global Alignment

The IFQ assumes that learners of similar age in different countries often occupy broadly comparable educational stages even when:

- curriculum content differs,
- pacing differs,
- terminology differs,
- or qualification systems differ.

Example:

- a learner moving from Ireland to Spain,
- or from the United States to France,

may encounter different academic expectations despite remaining at approximately the same educational progression stage.

The IFQ exists to support approximate alignment between such systems.

---

# 3. Relationship Between IFQ and Competency

The IFQ does not define learner competency.

Instead:

| Concept | Meaning |
|---|---|
| IFQ | Approximate educational progression stage |
| Competency | Actual learner capability/mastery |
| Lesson IFQ | Intended educational audience |
| Qualification | Formal educational outcome |

This separation is intentional.

It allows:

- gifted learners,
- delayed learners,
- adult learners,
- displaced learners,
- homeschooled learners,
- and learners from different systems

to interact with educational content flexibly.

---

# 4. Structural Design Principles

## 4.1 School-Age Education

During compulsory and school-age education:

- IFQ progression is primarily age-normalised.

This allows comparison between:

- year groups,
- grade systems,
- and national educational structures.

The framework assumes:

- approximate educational progression by age,

while acknowledging that curriculum coverage may vary significantly.

---

## 4.2 Post-Secondary and Tertiary Education

After compulsory education:

- IFQ progression becomes increasingly qualification-stage-normalised.

This reflects the reality that:

- university participation,
- degree duration,
- postgraduate study,
- and adult-entry pathways

vary significantly internationally.

At tertiary levels, IFQ alignment is informed more strongly by:

- qualification stage,
- ISCED alignment,
- and international higher-education conventions.

---

## 4.3 Lessons and Learning Objectives

Educational content may be independently classified with IFQ levels.

This enables:

- lesson recommendation,
- learner matching,
- adaptive educational systems,
- curriculum comparison,
- and educational discovery.

Example:

| Lesson | Recommended IFQ |
|---|---|
| Basic Fractions | IFQ 6 |
| Introductory Algebra | IFQ 10 |
| Differential Calculus | IFQ 16 |

The lesson IFQ represents:

> the approximate educational progression stage for which the content is suitable.

It does not guarantee:

- mastery,
- readiness,
- or curriculum equivalence.

---

# 5. International Standard Alignment

## 5.1 ISCED

The IFQ is intended to align broadly with the UNESCO International Standard Classification of Education (ISCED).

ISCED acts as:

- the international structural backbone for educational-stage comparison.

However:

- IFQ provides finer practical granularity for instructional targeting and recommendation.

ISCED alignment is therefore:

- approximate,
- supportive,
- and non-authoritative.

# 6. Recommended Workbook Structure

The IFQ workbook should be separated into distinct conceptual layers.

---

## 6.1 IFQ Core Sheet

Canonical IFQ progression model.

Suggested fields:

| Field | Purpose |
|---|---|
| IFQ Level | Canonical IFQ identifier |
| Typical Age | Approximate progression age |
| ISCED Alignment | Broad UNESCO mapping |
| Broad Stage | Human-readable stage |
| Notes | Structural guidance |

---

## 6.2 Country Mapping Sheet

Maps national educational systems into IFQ.

Suggested fields:

| Field | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Country | National system |
| Local Stage Name | Year/grade/stage |
| IFQ Level | Mapped IFQ |
| Typical Age | Typical learner age |
| Notes | System-specific exceptions |

---

## 6.3 Qualification Mapping Sheet

Maps formal qualifications and outcomes.

Suggested fields:

| Field | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Qualification | Exam/degree/certificate |
| Country/System | Origin system |
| IFQ Level | Approximate placement |
| ISCED | International alignment |
| Notes | Qualification context |

---

## 6.4 Lesson Classification Sheet

Maps educational content to IFQ.

Suggested fields:

| Field | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Lesson/Content | Educational material |
| Subject | Subject area |
| Recommended IFQ | Intended audience |
| Prerequisites | Optional dependency guidance |
| Notes | Additional context |

---

## 6.5 Metadata and Exceptions Sheet

Captures structural irregularities and exceptions.

Examples include:

- Transition Year,
- vocational pathways,
- integrated masters,
- apprenticeships,
- accelerated progression,
- adult-return pathways,
- and non-standard educational structures.

---

# 7. Future Considerations

The current IFQ model is intentionally general-purpose.

Future versions may consider:

- subject-specific progression,
- competency overlays,
- adaptive recommendation systems,
- multilingual educational alignment,
- curriculum graphing,
- and AI-assisted learner progression.

However:

the core IFQ should remain:

- simple,
- internationally comparable,
- human-readable,
- and recommendation-friendly.

---

# 8. Non-Goals

The IFQ is NOT intended to:

- replace national qualifications frameworks,
- determine immigration equivalence,
- certify competency,
- replace curriculum authorities,
- or provide legal educational recognition.

It is designed to support:

> educational comparison and instructional recommendation.

---

# 9. Guiding Principle

The IFQ exists to help learners access educational material at an approximately appropriate progression stage regardless of:

- nationality,
- educational background,
- institutional pathway,
- or life circumstance.

The framework prioritises:

- accessibility,
- comparability,
- educational portability,
- and learner inclusion.
